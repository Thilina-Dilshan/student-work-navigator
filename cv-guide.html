Quick Help & Guides

Start with the right information — three focused guides to help you go from “where do I start” to a finished application.

Beginner-Friendly Job Recommendations

If you've never worked in Germany before, it's easiest to start with roles that don't require German fluency or a large network. Good starting points for most first-semester international students:

  • Campus roles — library assistant, cafeteria/Mensa staff, IT helpdesk, event support. Usually English-friendly and used to working with students.
  • HiWi positions in your own department — ask professors directly after class or by email; many roles are never formally advertised.
  • Retail & delivery Minijobs — higher availability, flexible shifts, low barrier to entry.
  • Tutoring — if you're strong in maths, programming, or a language, platforms and university notice boards often have demand.

As your German improves and you build a network, Werkstudent roles in your field become realistic and pay significantly better.

CV & Application Guide

German-style applications are more structured than in many other countries. A few conventions to follow:

  • Lebenslauf (CV): reverse-chronological, 1–2 pages, includes education, work experience, skills, languages (with CEFR level, e.g. English C1, German B1) and sometimes a photo.
  • Anschreiben (cover letter): one page, structured as: why this company, why this role, why you — specific, not generic.
  • Formatting: clean, consistent fonts, no spelling mistakes, saved as a single PDF where possible.
  • Language: apply in the language of the job posting — many student jobs at international companies accept English.

Your university's Career Service usually offers free CV reviews — it's worth booking a session before your first application.

Work-Study Compatibility

Balancing work and studies in Germany comes with real legal limits, especially for non-EU students:

  • 120 full days or 240 half days per year is the general cap on paid work for non-EU international students (student visa holders). Werkstudent-classified roles are the main exception during term.
  • 20 hours/week is the informal ceiling recommended during lecture periods so work doesn't hurt your studies or your visa status — you can typically work more during semester breaks.
  • EU/EEA students generally have fewer restrictions but should still check local rules.
  • Always confirm your personal limits with your university's International Office or the local Ausländerbehörde (immigration office) — rules can change and depend on your specific visa.

Track your work days from day one — a simple spreadsheet with dates and hours will save you stress at visa renewal time.
