Job Types

Understand Job Types in Germany

Four common ways international students work legally in Germany while studying. Each has different rules on hours, pay and how closely it needs to relate to your studies.

HiWi / Research Assistant (Wissenschaftliche Hilfskraft)

A HiWi job is a position at your own university, usually inside a research group, chair, institute or library. You support academic staff with tasks like literature research, data analysis, preparing teaching materials, lab work, or programming — often tied to your field of study.

Because it's run directly by the university, contracts and pay rates are standardised, and the working hours are designed to fit around lectures.

Typically 5–10 hrs/week Related to your studies Employer: the university

Good for: students who want research experience, closer contact with professors, or a CV line directly tied to their degree.

Where to find: OVGU HiWi vacancy lists, notice boards at your faculty, or by directly emailing professors whose courses you enjoyed.

Werkstudent (Working Student)

A Werkstudent role is a part-time job at a company — from local Mittelstand firms to large employers with offices near Magdeburg — specifically designed for enrolled students. It's one of the best ways to gain real industry experience while studying, and often leads to a full-time offer after graduation.

German law caps Werkstudent contracts at 20 hours/week during term time (more is allowed during semester breaks), and importantly, these hours don't count against the 120-day limit for non-EU students.

Max 20 hrs/week in term Doesn't use up your 120-day limit Career-building

Good for: students who want professional, CV-relevant experience and a pathway to future full-time employment.

Minijob

A Minijob is a small, flexible job — think retail, hospitality, delivery, or campus catering — with earnings capped at €556/month (2025 threshold, check current limit). It's exempt from most taxes and social security contributions, making it simple to start.

Minijobs are easy to combine with studies but do count fully against the 120 full-day / 240 half-day annual work limit for non-EU international students, so track your days carefully.

Fast & flexible to start Income capped monthly Counts toward your 120-day limit

Good for: students who want quick extra income without a long field-related search, or who are just arriving and need a job fast.

Internship / Praktikum

Internships are short-term placements — sometimes required by your degree program (Pflichtpraktikum), sometimes optional (freiwilliges Praktikum) — that let you explore a career path in depth. They can be paid or unpaid, though internships longer than 3 months are usually entitled to at least minimum wage.

Mandatory internships written into your study regulations don't count against the international-student work-day limit; voluntary ones generally do.

Career exploration Paid or unpaid Mandatory internships are exempt from the limit

Good for: students who want to test a specific industry or role before committing to it after graduation.
